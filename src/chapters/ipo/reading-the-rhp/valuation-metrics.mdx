# Valuation Metrics

Understanding IPO valuation helps you decide if the price is fair. This section explains how to evaluate if an IPO is reasonably priced.

<InfoBox type="note">
  The "Basis for Issue Price" section in the RHP shows how the company justifies its valuation. Learn to verify these claims independently.
</InfoBox>

## The Basis for Issue Price

Located in the RHP, this section shows:
- EPS (basic and diluted)
- P/E ratio at price band
- NAV per share
- Peer comparison
- Qualitative factors

## Price-to-Earnings (P/E) Ratio

The most common valuation metric:

**P/E = Issue Price / Earnings Per Share**

How to use it:
1. Find post-IPO EPS (on fully diluted basis)
2. Calculate P/E at upper and lower band
3. Compare with listed peers

<Table 
  headers={["P/E vs Peers","Interpretation"]}
  rows={[
    ["Lower P/E", "Potentially undervalued"],
    ["Similar P/E", "Fairly priced"],
    ["Higher P/E", "Premium pricing – needs justification"]
  ]}
/>

<InfoBox type="tip">
  Always use diluted EPS (includes all potential shares). Basic EPS can be misleading in IPOs.
</InfoBox>

## Which EPS to Use?

Options:
- Last full year EPS
- Trailing 12 months (TTM)
- Annualized based on recent quarters

RHP typically shows multiple scenarios. Use the most recent for current valuation.

## Price-to-Book (P/B) Ratio

Relevant for asset-heavy companies:

**P/B = Issue Price / Net Asset Value per Share**

<Table 
  headers={["P/B Value","Interpretation"]}

    ["1-2", "Reasonable for manufacturing"],
    ["2-5", "Premium, common for good companies"],
    ["More than 5", "High premium – growth expectations"]
  ]}
/>

## Price-to-Sales Ratio

For growth companies not yet profitable:

**P/S = Market Cap at Issue Price / Annual Revenue**

Useful for:
- Tech companies with no profits
- High-growth businesses reinvesting everything
- Companies investing heavily in customer acquisition

<InfoBox type="important">
  P/S ignores profitability. A company might have high sales but terrible unit economics. Always check margins too.
</InfoBox>

For comparing companies with different capital structures:

**EV = Market Cap + Debt - Cash**

### Finding Real Comparables

Don't rely only on the RHP:
- Search for listed competitors
- Check sector indices
- Look at global peers

<InfoBox type="warning">
  Companies pick flattering comparisons. If they compare with a 50 P/E company, check if that comparison is valid.
</InfoBox>

## Growth Adjustment

Higher growth deserves higher valuation:

**PEG = P/E / Earnings Growth Rate**

<Table 
  headers={["PEG","Interpretation"]}
  rows={[
    ["Less than 1", "Possibly undervalued"],
    ["1", "Fair value"],
    ["More than 1", "Possibly overvalued"]
  ]}
/>

Example:
- P/E of 40 with 40% growth = PEG of 1 (Fair)
- P/E of 40 with 20% growth = PEG of 2 (Expensive)

## Key Takeaways

- P/E is the primary valuation metric for most IPOs
- Compare with listed peers using same metrics
- Higher growth can justify higher valuations
- Use EV multiples for capital structure differences
- Be skeptical of the peer selection in the RHP
